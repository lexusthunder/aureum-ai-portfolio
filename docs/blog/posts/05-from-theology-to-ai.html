From theology to AI engineering: the only pivot that worked

I have a Bachelor's in Pastoral Theology. I'm applying to Google AI Engineer roles. Here's how I closed the credibility gap — and what I'd do differently if I started over today.

I'm going to skip the inspirational arc. Here's the honest version.

I have a Bachelor's in Pastoral Theology from Babeș-Bolyai University. For most of my early career I drove for Amazon Logistics, packed produce in the Netherlands, ran resort operations in California. I cooked the books for nobody. I wrote zero code professionally until I was 27.

Today I'm in interview prep for Google AI Engineer. The portfolio I'm pointing at has 16 production-style AI/ML projects, a full-stack SaaS I founded, and an autonomous AI software engineer in the same archetype as Cognition Labs and Cursor.

Three things made the pivot work. Three things I would do differently.

What worked

(1) I built things, not credentials

The temptation when pivoting is to credentialise — bootcamps, certifications, MicroMasters, paid Coursera tracks. I did some of these and then stopped. Nobody at Google has ever asked me about a certificate. They've asked about projects.

The first thing I did that mattered was ship a multi-agent Claude pipeline that produced typed business reports. Pydantic schemas, retry logic, structured logging. ~10 files. It didn't get me a job — but it got me into conversations with engineers, and those conversations got me to the next project, and the project after that. Compounding.

(2) I leveraged what I already had

Theology training is, surprise, extremely useful for prompt engineering. You spend years learning to parse loaded text, structure arguments, write clear prose, hold contradictory positions in your head. Those skills transfer 1:1 to LLM work — the entire game is "phrase this such that the model interprets it correctly".

I didn't try to hide the theology degree. I framed it. "I spent four years learning to read complicated text carefully and structure arguments — that's what prompt engineering is." Stopped a few interviewers cold. In a good way.

(3) I built a brand, not a job application

Most pivoters apply to 200 jobs and get 4 callbacks. I applied to fewer. Instead I built Aureum — a portfolio with my own narrative ("I'm building Genesis, in the same space as Cognition"), a public site, a blog, a sales kit. Recruiters don't filter that. They engage with it.

What I'd do differently

(1) Start the public artefact on day 1

I waited 18 months to put anything public. The compound returns of public work — GitHub commits, a blog, a portfolio site — start at month 0. Every day you wait is a day you're not in someone's search results.

If you're pivoting today: ship a GitHub repo this week. Even if it's empty. Plant the flag.

(2) Pick one wedge, not five

I tried to be "an AI engineer who can also do mobile, also full-stack, also data science, also DevOps". That made my CV legible to no one. Better positioning: pick one — autonomous agents, MLOps, computer vision, NLP — go deep, become known for it, then expand.

If I were starting today, my wedge would be "autonomous coding agents". That's where the talent compounds fastest in 2026.

(3) Don't confuse "applying" with "getting hired"

The cold-application funnel converts at ~1%. A referral converts at ~30%. 30× difference. The single highest-leverage thing a pivoter can do is get one warm intro per week. Conferences, Discord, Twitter, GitHub issues — there are a hundred ways to be in the same room as someone who works at the company you want.

The honest part

The pivot took longer than I want to admit. There were months where it felt pointless. There were applications I never even got a "we've reviewed your application" email back from. There were take-homes where I bombed the system-design portion because I had never built a real system at scale.

The thing that kept it moving was small wins, recorded publicly. A new project shipped, even a small one. A blog post even three people read. A GitHub star from a stranger. The receipts compound, even when the inbox is empty.

If you're pivoting today

Three things, in order:

  1. Make a public GitHub. Today. Doesn't matter what's in it — make it.
  2. Pick one wedge. Build one thing in that wedge. Show your work in public.
  3. Find one warm intro per week. Comment on issues, attend meetups, message people whose work you admire. The funnel is human.

That's the entire playbook. The hard part is doing it for 18 consecutive months while your cohort thinks you're delusional.
